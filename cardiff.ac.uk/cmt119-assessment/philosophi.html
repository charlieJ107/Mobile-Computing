Philosophy of Computer Scicence

What is Computational Thinking and what does it have to do with some philosophical ideas?

The computer is called The Greatest machine that never was. Computer science, the closest discipline to computers, has enriched its connotation and strengthened its energy in hundreds of years of development since the Industrial Revolution. Nowadays, computer science has become an independent, profound and still developing discipline and releases more power in the intersection with other fields.

Alan Turing, the founder of computer science, asked, "Can machines think?" It has been quoted so many times that many people confuse it with the question, "Can a machine think like a human". Nowadays, even with the development of computer science for nearly a hundred years, machines are still unable to think like human beings. On the contrary, many people devote themselves to researching how to control the machine better. When we face a variety of problems in research, people have to sum up a series of methodologies that are very useful in problem modelling or the design of operation steps and condense it into a mode of thinking. People find that we can constantly get better results when we master such thinking methods and use these methods to understand computers and make better use of computers. This method is called Computational Thinking. From the meaning of the word philosophy, this is the philosophy of computers.

Although Computational Thinking includes Decomposition, Abstraction, Pattern Recognition and Algorithm Design in terms of concepts explained by many materials, from a philosophical point of view, I have found many philosophical viewpoints that are consistent with Computational Thinking. In the next part of the article, I will explain the connotation of Computational Thinking from these philosophical points of view.

Reductionism

If we go deep into the world of computer science, We will find that most people are reductionists. The Turing machine, which can calculate everything computable, is one of the most typical reductionist machines. People with computational thinking believe a complex system can be modelled precisely and exactly by a few simple principles or a combination and interaction of several subsystems. Conversely, if you cannot break things apart like a reductionist, you may have a miserable life in computer science. An example of this is simply the title image of this passage.

For someone who does not have Computational thought, the fact that "the equals sign assigns the value on the right to the left" might be so simple that they may want to move on to the next topic as soon as possible. Such a person may find that people like programmer seems to like to show off some introductory knowledge of some disciplines or extremely bizarre concepts regardless of the occasion. Programmers seem always entangled in some shallow and uninteresting introductory knowledge rather than confronting real problems. They will even get annoyed and say, "We are talking about real issues, and if you do not understand anything, you cannot talk about it". However, to people with Computational Thinking, this introductory knowledge, like the book of Genesis, underlies all the following discussions. They don't like those high-level assertions that have no origin. Instead, they start from the most uncomplicated, primary, and improbable things and construct and derive high-level topics on their initiative.

Learning in the field or communicating with a machine smoothly is more manageable if someone understands reductionism. Moreover, there will be fewer bugs in writing code. It may be difficult for such a person to become a qualified practitioner in the computer field and even more challenging to achieve achievements in this area.

Systems Theory

Suppose reductionism opens the black boxes of problems. In that case, systems theory tries to encapsulate the low-level details into black boxes and make problems easier to solve based on these functions. Computer science is an engineering discipline. Just as it is impossible to disassemble a complete car just by disassembling the parts, it is necessary to start with the most fundamental components, design a suitable structure, and assemble them into a finished product.

Any qualified computer science practitioner must have at least some systems thinking. After we use the idea of reductionism to implement more complex operations with the most uncomplicated process, we need to use system theory to combine complex procedures into a complete system. We need to consider what this function needs to pass in, what it will give back, and how much it can guarantee that it will not go wrong. Suppose a practitioner cannot understand these, he may be able to stay on simple introductory programs with only a few lines of code. More than that is required for more complex programs or systems, which are usually complex enough to solve real-world problems. It can be seen that system theory is an essential part of Computational Thinking and one of the critical cornerstones guiding the development of computer science.

Algorithm & Pattern Recognition

I intended to use the concept of Control Theory to describe the philosophy used in Computational Thinking to design a series of steps for machines and to understand and abstract existing control processes. However, with further research, cybernetics can only well summarize the ideological connotation of a computer in the parts of Algorithm and Pattern Recognition.

Computer science is the science that revolves around the most exquisite machine in human history. To control this machine well, we must guide our process according to a set of theories. As a complex system, if there is a slight difference in the control signal of any part, it will bring severe and complicated consequences. This feature means we must carefully design each control signal and handle every feedback output from the system. Although many scholars have given different definitions of the word algorithm, it always expresses the same meaning to a certain extent. In a broad sense, some specified steps to do some operations to achieve a specific purpose can be regarded as an algorithm. Because computer science revolves around the computer, a loyal, reliable and complex machine, when the algorithm we set, or the operation procedure, is accurate and robust enough, we can ensure that the computer works according to our requirements.

For a computer science practitioner, the algorithm is not only a work step to be designed when communicating with the computer but also an abstraction of the solution. Algorithms enable us to be abstract, encapsulate the patterns of a series of simple operational processes into reusable steps, and split the problem into these reusable steps when we encounter more complex problems. It also enables us to organize solutions exquisitely and efficiently and build solutions to new problems using existing knowledge of solutions to other issues. Suppose someone needs to gain the ability and expertise in this area, which means they cannot model a real problem as a problem that can be executed by a computer and then cannot give the computer the correct instructions to solve it. This is unimaginable in the research and work in computer science.

Conclusion

Although a simple enumeration of several ideas does not generalize the whole meaning of Computational Thinking, Computational Thinking contains considerable guidance from these philosophical ideas. It refines the specific problems in computer science. Computational thinking is not a simple combination of ideas but the product of complex chemical reactions of many different modes of thinking in one discipline. It is also the internal driving force to constantly improve and enrich itself in the course of nearly a hundred years of development of this discipline. Mastering Computational Thinking and using it to guide the study and work in the field of computer science is necessary for further in-depth study of computer science.
