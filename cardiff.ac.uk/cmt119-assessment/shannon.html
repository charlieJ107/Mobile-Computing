Computational Thinking

By Zhuoling Jiang

Claude Shannon

A scientist, a mathematician and an engineer
Estate of Francis Bello / Science Source

Science seeks the basic laws of nature. Mathematics searches for new theorems to build upon the old. Engineering builds systems to solve human needs. The three disciplines are interdependent but distinct. Very rarely does one individual simultaneously make central contributions to all three — but Claude Shannon was a rare individual.

Shannon was born in Gaylord, Michigan, in 1916, his parents are a local businessman and a teacher. After earning a double bachelor's degree in electrical engineering and mathematics from the University of Michigan, Shannon entered the Massachusetts Institute of Technology and began her graduate studies.

In his master thesis, he applied the mathematical method of Boolean algebra to the analysis and synthesis of switching circuits in the electronic field. At the time, electromagnetic relays were used to build circuits that routed telephone calls or controlled complex machines. However, The way people thought about them was in terms of the relay coils being energized or not. And there was no consistent theory on how to design or analyze such circuits. Shannon used Boolean algebra of logic to analyze, and then synthesize, switching circuits and to prove that the overall circuit worked as desired. And he invented the AND, OR, and NOT logic gates, upon wich the entire edifice of computer science is based.

His most frequently mentioned achievement is information theory, which he calls "grand unified theory for communication" in a letter to his mentor in in 1939. The core of the theory proposed by Shannon is a simple but universal communication model: the transmitter encodes the information into a signal, which is affected by noise and then decoded by the receiver. Although the model is simple, it contains two key elements: the signal source and noise source are separated from the communication system to be designed, and the probabilistic modeling of the two sources is carried out respectively.

A schematic diagram of Shannon’s model of communication, as taken from his paper. From: The Bell System Technical Journal

Shannon creatively applied probability to the measurement of information, transforming the communication problem from a physical problem to a mathematical problem. This is quite different from the previous common method of treating the communication problem as the reconstruction problem of deterministic signals. It is this assumption that allows Shannon to use probability to model uncertainty. And uncertainty is the charm of communication.

"After all, if you knew ahead of time what I would say to you in this column, what would be the point of writing it?"

Shannon gave a formula for the minimum number of bits per second to represent the information, a number he called its entropy rate, H Then he provide a formula for the maximum number of bits per second that can be reliably communicated in the face of noise, which he called the system’s capacity, C. Finally, he showed that reliable communication of the information from the source in the face of noise is possible if and only if H < C While this is a theory of communication, it is, at the same time, a theory of how information is produced and transferred — an information theory. Thus Shannon is now considered “the father of information theory.”

Shannon's theory drew some conclusions, which directly led to the development of the whole communication field. The 5G technology we use today is the two sets of coding methods proposed by people after continuous exploration, which can get fast communication at the same time of given reliability, so as to reach the theoretical speed limit.

Shannon is a scientist, whose theory is as fundamental as the laws of physics; Shannon is also a mathematician, who has created new mathematical concepts to explain communication principles, and these ideas have been widely used in other branches of mathematics; Shannon is even an engineer, whose theory is inspired by practical engineering problems, and this theory has become the basic framework of modern communication systems.
